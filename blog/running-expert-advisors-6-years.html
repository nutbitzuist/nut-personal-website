The Reality of Running Expert Advisors for 6 Years

Nut (Sarunporn)
10 min read

Six years ago, I deployed my first Expert Advisor on a live account. I thought I was prepared. I had backtested it across 10 years of data. The equity curve looked beautiful. The Sharpe ratio was excellent. I was ready.

I wasn't ready. Not even close. Here's what I've learned since then.

Backtesting Is Not Reality

This is the hardest lesson every EA trader learns. Backtesting gives you confidence. Live trading gives you humility.

In backtesting, you have perfect order fills. No slippage. No requotes. No server disconnections at 3 AM when your EA tries to close a position during NFP. The gap between backtest results and live results is where most EA traders lose their sanity — and their capital.

After 6 years, I've learned to expect a 20-30% degradation from backtest to live performance. If your backtest shows 50% annual return, plan for 35-40% in reality. If that's still acceptable, you have a viable strategy. If not, go back to the drawing board.

Drawdowns Will Test You

The biggest psychological challenge isn't losses. It's sustained drawdowns. A bad day is nothing. A bad week is uncomfortable. But a bad month — watching your EA bleed capital slowly, day after day, while following the rules perfectly? That's where most people break.

"The market can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent." — Keynes

I've had drawdowns that lasted 3 months. Three months of watching the account go down, trusting the math, trusting the system. And then it recovered. Every single time — so far.

The key insights about drawdowns:

  • Size your positions for the worst case. If your backtest shows a 20% max drawdown, plan for 40%. Markets will always surprise you with new extremes.
  • Diversify across strategies. Multiple uncorrelated EAs smooth the equity curve. What kills you with one EA might be a non-event across a portfolio.
  • Set clear rules for intervention. Define in advance: at what drawdown level do you pause the EA? At what level do you reduce position size? Don't make these decisions when you're emotional.

The Infrastructure Matters More Than the Strategy

Beginners obsess over entry signals. Veterans obsess over infrastructure.

In 6 years, I've had more losses from infrastructure failures than from bad strategies:

  • VPS crashes during volatile markets
  • Broker changing spread widening rules without notice
  • Internet disconnections causing orphaned positions
  • MT4/MT5 updates breaking EA compatibility
  • Timezone changes affecting session-based logic

Today, I run redundant VPS instances across different providers. I have monitoring dashboards that alert me within seconds of any anomaly. I use multiple brokers to reduce single-point-of-failure risk. The boring stuff is what keeps the lights on.

What I Look for in an EA Today

After 6 years of building, testing, deploying, and killing EAs, here's what I prioritize:

  1. Simplicity. The best EAs have the fewest parameters. If an EA needs 20 optimized inputs to work, it's curve-fitted and will fail in live.
  2. Robustness. Does it work across multiple pairs? Multiple timeframes? If it only works on EURUSD H1 with exact parameters, it's fragile.
  3. Reasonable expectations. I aim for 20-40% annual return with max 15% drawdown. Not sexy, but sustainable. Over years, that compounds massively.
  4. Risk management first. The entry signal is maybe 20% of the system. The other 80% is position sizing, stop loss, take profit, and portfolio-level risk controls.

Was It Worth It?

Absolutely. But not for the reasons you might think.

The additional cashflow is great. Having trading robots that generate income while I sleep, while I work my day job, while I spend time with family — that's real leverage.

But the bigger win is what the process taught me. Building EAs forced me to think in systems. To be ruthlessly honest about what works and what doesn't. To separate process from outcome. To be patient. To trust the math over the emotions.

Those skills transfer to everything — business, investing, personal development. The EA is just a vehicle. The real product is the mindset.
